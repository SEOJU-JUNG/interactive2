Giant Plastic island
floating in the
Pacific Ocean?

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP) is the largest of the five offshore plastic accumulation zones in the world, located between East-Asia countries and the USA. The GPGP covers an estimated surface area of 1.6 million Km², an area 7 times the size of Korea. According to UNEA, the United Nations Environment Programme, 90% of ocean garbage is coming from only ten rivers in the world, and 7 of them are in Asian countries. As one of the Asian countries, We can't deny that Korea has contributed to the Ocean Garbage issues because it is one of the highest plastic consumption countries, annually exporting the vast amount of plastic waste to other Asian countries.

To prevent further damage, many countries across the world have been intensifying plastic laws as a global trend. South Korea also should take responsibility for the issue and tighten plastic laws. Otherwise, it will severely impact our ecosystems, health, and economies.

We propose
3 new plastic laws.

PACKAGING

Intensify laws on plastic packaging; Forbid the provision of disposable products for free for delivery.

CARE PRODUCTS

Prohibit the use of microplastics in the manufacturing of cosmetics and personal care products.

STYROFOAM

Forbid styrofoam buoys used in fish farms and provide free eco-friendly buoys instead.

Our Research

Our research shows the limitations of the current plastic laws in South Korea
and demonstrates the need to strengthen the plastic laws we suggest above.

S.Korea is one of the
world's biggest
plastic consumers.

In 2019, South Korea consumed 144 kg of plastic per capita, surpassing both the US and China at 99kg and 63 kg. More than half of the plastic waste from South Korea is composed of packaging. The overflowing plastics coming from delivery food is one of the significant contributors — According to the Ministry of Environment, delivery service is estimated at more than 1.5 million, using at least three disposable containers at a time. Delivery alone providers 4.5 million plastic waste on a daily basis.
Kim, a member of the Greenpeace, stated, "Consumers can bear the discomfort and reduce their use of single-use cups and straws, but a greater portion of plastic production and waste are created by industry packaging. It's time for the industry to reduce plastic." Undoubtedly, plastic laws should be strengthened to increase corporate responsibility for the environment and prevent free disposable plastic packaging in delivering service.

microplastics are
silent killers

Plastic debris less than five millimeters in length is classified as microplastics. Microbeads are considered as microplastics, showing mostly in personal care products such as face soap, body wash, toothpaste, etc. Companies use the microbeads as an exfoliant or just for texture in their products.
Microbeads are so small that most of them end up in our waterways. These tiny bits of plastic act like sponges, soaking up the toxins around them. So, a single microbead can end up being a million times more toxic than the water around it. These toxins come into our meals once ocean species eat them.

S.Korea's the highest
microplastic
pollution level.

South Korea's ocean microplastic pollution level is almost the highest in the world. Researchers at the University of Manchester in the U.K reported that the Merge and Irwell rivers in the U.K have the highest microplastic concentration in the world. Korea's Incheon coast and Nakdong River estuary were ranked second and third. "Styrofoam buoys contribute to the level of microplastic density on the coast of Korea,"
said Dr. Hong Sun-wook, a header of the East Asian Sea Community. There are many fish farms in the West and South sea in Korea. Most of the buoys used in these farms are styrofoam, polluting the water. Debris piles on the coastline and leads to the concentrations of microplastics when these buoys become damaged.
