Giant Plastic island
floating in the
Pacific Ocean?

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP) is the largest of the five offshore plastic accumulation zones in the world. The GPGP covers an estimated surface area of 1.6 million Km², an area seven times the size of S.Korea. Located between East-Asia countries and the USA, it eventually returns to us and harmfully impacts on our health.
To against further damage, many countries, including China, the USA, and Japan, have been intensifying the plastic laws. Korea also should take responsibility for the issue and tighten plastic laws. Otherwise, it will severely impact our ecosystems, health, and economies.

S.Korea is one of the
world's biggest
plastic consumers.

In 2019, South Korea consumed 144 kg of plastic per capita, surpassing both the US and China at 99kg and 63 kg. And, more than half of the plastic waste from South Korea is composed of packaging. The overflowing plastic package coming from the delivery food is one of the biggest reasons —Daily orders for delivery apps are estimated at more than 1.5 million, using at least three disposable containers at a time. Delivery alone providers 4.5 million plastic waste daily.
According to Greenpeace's Kim, "Consumers can bear the discomfort and reduce their use of single-use cups and straws, but a greater portion of plastic production and waste is created by industry and their packaging. It's time for the industry to reduce plastic." Undoubtedly, plastic laws should be strengthened to increase corporate responsibility for environmental pollution further and prevent the provision of disposable products for free when delivering or take out.

microplastics are
silent killers

Plastic debris that is less than five millimeters in length (or about the size of a sesame seed) are called microplastics. Microbeads are in a lot of stuff, but they show upmost in personal care products. — Face soap, body wash, toothpaste, etc. Companies put them in these products as an exfoliant or just for texture. They are so small that most of them end up in our waterways every day. These tiny bits of plastic act like sponges, soaking up the toxins around them. So, a single microbead can end up being a million times more toxic than the water around it, which comes back to our meal once they get eaten by fish and other animals.
