Giant Plastic island
floating in the
Pacific Ocean?

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch (GPGP) is the largest of the five offshore plastic accumulation zones in the world’s oceans. The GPGP covers an estimated surface area of 1.6 million square kilometers, an area seven times the size of S.Korea. It is located between East-Asia countries and the USA, which eventually return back and harmfully impacting on us health.
To against for further damage, many countries, including China, the USA, and Japan, have been intensifying the plastic laws. Korea also should take responsibility for the issue and tighten plastic laws up to the level of other countries who have been practically implementing such laws. Otherwise, it will impact our ecosystems, health, and economies.

Our research and data shows the need for three strengthen

S.Korea is one of the
world's biggest
plastic consumers.

In 2015, it consumed 317 pounds (144 kilograms) of plastic per capita, surpassing both the US and China at 218 and 138 pounds (99kg and 63 kg), respectively, according to Euromap, an association for plastics and rubber machinery manufacturers. While South Korea last year banned single-use plastic bags from supermarkets, Greenpeace's Kim says more must be done. " Consumers can bear the discomfort and reduce their use of single-use cups and straws," she says. " But a greater portion of plastic production and waste is created by industry and their packaging. It's time for industry to reduce plastic.”

More than half of Korea's waste plastics are applied with packaging materials. The "Resource Recycling Act" regulates the provision of one-time goods free of charge, except for delivery or takeout. As you know Korea has delivered food culture do a lot. Daily orders for delivery apps are more than 1.5 million cases (estimated by the delivery industry), and 4.5 million plastic waste products are available every day for delivery alone. We need to revise the law to further increase the responsibility of environmental pollution by companies and prevent the provision of disposable products for free when delivering or taking out.

microplastics are
silent killers

Plastic debris that is less than five millimeters in length (or about the size of a sesame seed) are called microplastics. Microbeads are in a lot of stuff, but they show upmost in personal care products. — Face soap, body wash, toothpaste, etc. Companies put them in these products as an exfoliant or just for texture. They are so small that most of them end up in our waterways every day. These tiny bits of plastic act like sponges, soaking up the toxins around them. So, a single microbead can end up being a million times more toxic than the water around it, which comes back to our meal once they get eaten by fish and other animals.
